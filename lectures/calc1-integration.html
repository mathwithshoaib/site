§ 1 — The Big Question

You Know Derivatives.
Now, Let's Work Backwards.

You have spent weeks mastering derivatives — the tool that captures instantaneous rates of change. The derivative of position is velocity; the derivative of velocity is acceleration; the derivative of profit is marginal profit.

Now consider the reverse question — perhaps the most natural question in all of mathematics: if I know only the rate of change, can I recover the original quantity? This question, simple to ask and profound to answer, is the soul of integration.

🚗 A Road-Trip Problem

Your car's speedometer works perfectly, but the odometer is broken. You need to know: "How far have I traveled in the last 3 hours?"

You record your speed at half-hour intervals:

Time (hr)Speed (km/h)

2.565

Best estimate: multiply each speed by 0.5 hr and sum:
$(60+80+70+90+50+65)(0.5) = \mathbf{207.5 \text{ km}}$

But this is only an approximation. The speed changed continuously between measurements. Integration gives us the exact answer by considering infinitely many such measurements simultaneously.

The Core Insight: If $v(t)$ is speed at time $t$, then the exact total distance from $t=a$ to $t=b$ is the area under the curve $y=v(t)$ — and that area is what the integral computes.
§ 2 — Where Integration Comes From

Riemann Sums: The Birth
of the Integral Sign

Picture slicing the shaded region under a curve into a hundred paper-thin vertical strips. Each strip is almost a perfect rectangle — you know its width precisely, and its height is just the value of the function at one chosen point. Sum every rectangle's area, and you have a remarkably good approximation of the true area beneath the curve. Now imagine slicing into a thousand strips. A million. A trillion. Something extraordinary happens: as the strips grow infinitely thin, the approximation doesn't spin off to infinity or collapse to zero — it converges, with the certainty of mathematics, to a single perfect number. That number is the definite integral.

The elongated $\displaystyle\int$ symbol? Leibniz invented it in 1675 as a stretched letter S — for Summa, Latin for sum. And the $dx$ at the end? It is the ghost of $\Delta x$ as it shrinks to zero, whispering: "I am the infinitely thin width of each slice." Every time you write $\int f(x)\,dx$, you are commanding an infinite army of rectangles to march toward perfection.

  1. Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ equal sub-intervals, each of width $\Delta x = \dfrac{b-a}{n}$.
  2. Pick a sample point $x_i^*$ in each sub-interval (left, right, or midpoint).
  3. Build rectangles: width $\Delta x$, height $f(x_i^*)$.
  4. Sum the areas: $\displaystyle S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$.
  5. Take the limit $n \to \infty$ — rectangles become infinitely thin, sum becomes exact.

§ 3 — The Formal Definition

The Definite Integral

The limiting value of the Riemann sum as $n \to \infty$ is the definite integral:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \;=\; \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$$

5.1a — Antidifferentiation

Antidifferentiation

If differentiation is the art of finding rates of change, antidifferentiation is the art of running backwards — recovering a function from its derivative. It is the mathematical equivalent of watching a film in reverse.

Definition — Antiderivative

A function $F(x)$ is an antiderivative of $f(x)$ on an interval if

$$F'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \text{ on that interval.}$$

The process of finding antiderivatives is called antidifferentiation or integration.

Fundamental Property of Antiderivatives

Theorem

If $F(x)$ is an antiderivative of the continuous function $f(x)$, then every other antiderivative of $f(x)$ has the form

$$G(x) = F(x) + C$$

for some constant $C$. In other words, all antiderivatives of $f$ are identical up to an additive constant.

💡 Why? A Quick Proof

Let $F$ and $G$ both satisfy $F'=G'=f$. Define $H=G-F$. Then $H'=G'-F'=f-f=0$ everywhere. A function with zero derivative on an interval must be constant. So $G(x)-F(x)=C$, giving $G(x)=F(x)+C$. ∎

Concrete example: $F(x)=x^3$, $G(x)=x^3+7$, $H(x)=x^3-\pi$ all satisfy $F'=G'=H'=3x^2$. All are antiderivatives of $3x^2$.

5.1b — The Indefinite Integral & Its Notation

The Indefinite Integral

The notation for antidifferentiation was crafted by Leibniz in the 17th century and every symbol carries precise meaning. Let's dissect it:

The Indefinite Integral

If $F'(x) = f(x)$, the indefinite integral of $f$ is written:

$$\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C$$
SymbolNameMeaning
$\int$Integral signStretched "S" — Summa. Commands: "find the antiderivative."
$f(x)$IntegrandThe function to be antidifferentiated
$dx$DifferentialIdentifies the variable; it is the limit of $\Delta x \to 0$
$F(x)$AntiderivativeAny function whose derivative is $f(x)$
$C$Constant of integrationRepresents the entire family of antiderivatives

Unlike the definite integral $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ (a number), the indefinite integral is a family of functions.

🔍 Why + C Matters — and Why It Cancels in Definite Integrals

Consider $F(x)=x^2$, $G(x)=x^2+5$, $H(x)=x^2-3$. All have derivative $2x$. Without extra information (an initial condition) we cannot know which one we want — so $+C$ honestly represents this ambiguity.

In a definite integral: $\bigl[F(b)+C\bigr]-\bigl[F(a)+C\bigr]=F(b)-F(a)$. The $C$'s cancel perfectly. So in definite integrals we may freely drop $C$.

5.1c — Rules for Integrating Common Functions

Integration Rules

Every differentiation rule, run in reverse, yields an integration rule. We cover the four essential rules with worked examples for each.

Rule 1  —  The Constant Rule

$$\int k\,dx = kx + C$$

Since $\dfrac{d}{dx}(kx)=k$, the antiderivative of any constant $k$ is simply $kx$.

Example 1. $\quad\displaystyle\int 7\,dx = 7x + C$
Check: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(7x+C)=7$ ✓
Example 2. $\quad\displaystyle\int (-4)\,dx = -4x + C$
$\displaystyle\int 0\,dx = C$  (an arbitrary constant — any constant has derivative zero).

Rule 2  —  The Power Rule

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \qquad (n \neq -1)$$

Raise the exponent by 1, divide by the new exponent. This directly reverses the differentiation power rule.

Example 1. $\quad\displaystyle\int x^5\,dx = \frac{x^6}{6} + C$
Check: $\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left(\dfrac{x^6}{6}\right) = \dfrac{6x^5}{6} = x^5$ ✓
Example 2 (fractional exponent).
$\displaystyle\int \sqrt{x}\,dx = \int x^{1/2}\,dx = \frac{x^{3/2}}{3/2}+C = \frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+C$
Example 3 (negative exponent).
$\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x^3}\,dx = \int x^{-3}\,dx = \frac{x^{-2}}{-2}+C = -\frac{1}{2x^2}+C$
$n=\tfrac{3}{4}$, new exponent $=\tfrac{7}{4}$:
$\displaystyle\int x^{3/4}\,dx = \frac{x^{7/4}}{7/4}+C = \frac{4}{7}x^{7/4}+C$

Rule 3  —  The Logarithmic Rule

$$\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C \qquad (x \neq 0)$$

The power rule fails for $n=-1$ (it would give $x^0/0$, undefined). But since $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln|x|=\dfrac{1}{x}$, the antiderivative is $\ln|x|$. The absolute value handles $x<0$.

Example 1. $\quad\displaystyle\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x|+C$
Example 2. $\quad\displaystyle\int \frac{8}{x}\,dx = 8\ln|x|+C$

Rule 4  —  The Exponential Rule

$$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C \qquad \text{and} \qquad \int e^{kx}\,dx = \frac{e^{kx}}{k}+C \;\;(k\neq 0)$$

The function $e^x$ is its own derivative — and therefore its own antiderivative. The general form with $kx$ requires dividing by $k$ (reversing the chain rule).

Example 1. $\quad\displaystyle\int e^x\,dx = e^x+C$
Example 2. $\quad\displaystyle\int e^{3x}\,dx = \frac{e^{3x}}{3}+C$
Example 3. $\quad\displaystyle\int 5e^{-2x}\,dx = 5\cdot\frac{e^{-2x}}{-2}+C = -\frac{5}{2}e^{-2x}+C$
Algebraic Rules for Indefinite Integration
Constant Multiple Rule
$$\int k\cdot f(x)\,dx = k\int f(x)\,dx$$

A constant factor slides freely outside the integral sign.

Sum and Difference Rule
$$\int \bigl[f(x) \pm g(x)\bigr]\,dx = \int f(x)\,dx \;\pm\; \int g(x)\,dx$$

Integrate term by term. (Caution: there is no product or quotient rule for integration — those require other techniques.)

✏️ Example — Combining Multiple Rules

Find $\displaystyle\int\!\left(6x^2 - \frac{4}{x} + 3e^x\right)dx$.

$$= 6\int x^2\,dx - 4\int\frac{1}{x}\,dx + 3\int e^x\,dx$$ $$= 6\cdot\frac{x^3}{3} - 4\ln|x| + 3e^x + C$$ $$= \boxed{2x^3 - 4\ln|x| + 3e^x + C}$$

✏️ Example — Simplify Before Integrating

Find $\displaystyle\int\frac{x^3 - 4x}{x}\,dx$.

Simplify first (divide each term by $x$):

$$\int\frac{x^3-4x}{x}\,dx = \int(x^2-4)\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3}-4x+C$$

5.1d — Applications

Integration in Action

📈 Example — Finding a Function from Its Slope

A curve passes through $(1,\,4)$ and has slope function $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 2x + 1$. Find $f(x)$.

Step 1 — Integrate the slope function:

$$f(x) = \int(3x^2-2x+1)\,dx = x^3 - x^2 + x + C$$

Step 2 — Apply the initial condition $f(1)=4$:

$$4 = (1)^3-(1)^2+(1)+C = 1+C \;\Rightarrow\; C = 3$$ $$\boxed{f(x) = x^3 - x^2 + x + 3}$$

💰 Example — Total Cost from Marginal Cost

A firm's marginal cost is $C'(x)=3x^2-12x+14$ dollars/unit, with fixed costs of $\$35$. Find the total cost function.

$$C(x)=\int(3x^2-12x+14)\,dx = x^3-6x^2+14x+K$$

Fixed costs: $C(0)=35 \Rightarrow K=35$.

$$\boxed{C(x) = x^3 - 6x^2 + 14x + 35}$$

e.g. cost of 5 units: $C(5)=125-150+70+35=\$80$.

5.1e — Introduction to Differential Equations

Differential Equations

A differential equation (DE) is an equation involving an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives. They are the language in which physics, biology, chemistry, and economics write their deepest laws of nature.

Definition

A differential equation is an equation relating an unknown function $y$ and its derivatives. The order of a DE is the order of the highest derivative appearing.

Types of Differential Equations

TypeForm / ExampleNote

In Calculus I we focus exclusively on separable equations — those where we can algebraically move all $y$'s to one side and all $x$'s to the other.

Method — Separation of Variables

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = f(x)\cdot g(y)$:

  1. Separate: write $\dfrac{dy}{g(y)} = f(x)\,dx$
  2. Integrate both sides: $\displaystyle\int\frac{dy}{g(y)} = \int f(x)\,dx$
  3. Solve the resulting equation for $y$
  4. Apply any initial condition to determine $C$
📝 Why Separation of Variables Works

Think of $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as a ratio (Leibniz notation makes this rigorous). Multiplying both sides by $dx$ and dividing by $g(y)$ separates the variables. More formally: if $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$, divide both sides by $g(y)$ to get $\dfrac{1}{g(y)}\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)$. Integrating both sides with respect to $x$ and applying the substitution $dy=\dfrac{dy}{dx}\,dx$ converts the left side to $\int\dfrac{dy}{g(y)}$. The method is rigorously justified by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus and the Chain Rule.

💼 Initial Value Problem — Revenue from Marginal Revenue

A firm's marginal revenue satisfies $\dfrac{dR}{dq}=50-2q$, with $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$.

Separate and integrate:

$$dR = (50-2q)\,dq$$ $$R(q) = \int(50-2q)\,dq = 50q - q^2 + C$$

Apply $R(0)=0$: $\quad C=0$

$$\boxed{R(q)=50q-q^2}$$

Revenue is maximized when $R'(q)=50-2q=0 \Rightarrow q=25$ units; maximum revenue $=R(25)=\$625$.

🌱 Separable Equation — Exponential Growth/Decay

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=ky$ with initial condition $y(0)=y_0$.

Separate variables: $\dfrac{dy}{y}=k\,dx$

Integrate both sides: $\ln|y|=kx+C_1$

Exponentiate: $|y|=e^{C_1}e^{kx}$, so $y=Ce^{kx}$ ($C=\pm e^{C_1}$)

Apply IC: $y(0)=C=y_0$

$$\boxed{y=y_0 e^{kx}}$$

This is the universal model of exponential growth ($k>0$) and decay ($k<0$).

5.1f — Continuous Compounding via Differential Equations

Continuous Compounding

One of the most beautiful applications of differential equations is deriving the continuous compounding formula from first principles — not memorizing it, but building it from pure calculus logic.

🏦 Setting Up the Model

Suppose money in an account grows continuously at annual rate $r$. At every instant, the rate of growth of the balance $B(t)$ is proportional to the balance itself:

$$\frac{dB}{dt} = rB$$

This says: "The more money you have, the faster it grows." This is the fundamental differential equation of continuous compounding.

Theorem — Continuous Compounding Formula

Statement: If $P$ dollars are invested at annual interest rate $r$ (decimal form) compounded continuously, then the future value after $t$ years is

$$B(t) = Pe^{rt} \text{ dollars.}$$

✏️ Complete Derivation from the Differential Equation

Step 1 — State the IVP.

$$\frac{dB}{dt} = rB, \quad B(0)=P$$

Step 2 — Separate variables.

$$\frac{dB}{B} = r\,dt$$

Step 3 — Integrate both sides.

$$\int\frac{dB}{B} = \int r\,dt \;\Rightarrow\; \ln|B| = rt+C_1$$

Step 4 — Solve for $B$.

$$B(t) = e^{rt+C_1} = e^{C_1}\cdot e^{rt} = Ce^{rt}$$

Step 5 — Apply initial condition $B(0)=P$.

$$P = Ce^0 = C \;\Rightarrow\; C=P$$ $$\boxed{B(t) = Pe^{rt}}$$

Verification: $B'(t)=rPe^{rt}=r\cdot B(t)$ ✓   and   $B(0)=Pe^0=P$ ✓

💵 Numerical Example

$\$5{,}000$ is invested at $6\%$ annual interest compounded continuously. Find the balance after 10 years.

$$B(10) = 5000\cdot e^{0.06\times 10} = 5000\cdot e^{0.6} \approx 5000\times 1.8221 \approx \$9{,}110.60$$

The investment nearly doubled in 10 years.

§ 8 — Test Yourself

Practice Problems

Work each problem on paper first. Reveal the solution only when you are ready to check.

Problem 1 — Power Rule

Find $\displaystyle\int(4x^3-6x+5)\,dx$.

$$= 4\cdot\frac{x^4}{4}-6\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}+5x+C = \boxed{x^4-3x^2+5x+C}$$

Problem 2 — Mixed Rules

Find $\displaystyle\int\!\left(3\sqrt{x}-\frac{5}{x}+2e^x\right)dx$.

$$= 3\cdot\frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}-5\ln|x|+2e^x+C = \boxed{2x^{3/2}-5\ln|x|+2e^x+C}$$

Problem 3 — Initial Value Problem

Given $f'(x)=6x-4$ and $f(2)=3$, find $f(x)$.

$$f(x)=3x^2-4x+C$$ $$f(2)=12-8+C=3 \Rightarrow C=-1$$ $$\boxed{f(x)=3x^2-4x-1}$$

Problem 4 — Marginal Revenue

$R'(q)=120-4q$, $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$ and the revenue-maximizing quantity.

$$R(q)=120q-2q^2, \quad C=0$$

$R'(q)=0 \Rightarrow q=30$; maximum revenue $R(30)=3600-1800=\$1{,}800$.

Problem 5 — Continuous Compounding

How many years for $\$2{,}000$ to triple at $8\%$ continuous compounding?

$$6000=2000e^{0.08t} \Rightarrow e^{0.08t}=3 \Rightarrow t=\frac{\ln 3}{0.08}\approx\boxed{13.73 \text{ years}}$$

Problem 6 — Challenge: Derive from a Differential Equation

A population $P(t)$ grows according to $\dfrac{dP}{dt}=0.05P$ with $P(0)=1000$. Find $P(t)$ and the population after 20 years.

Separable: $\dfrac{dP}{P}=0.05\,dt \Rightarrow \ln P = 0.05t+C_1 \Rightarrow P=Ce^{0.05t}$

$P(0)=1000 \Rightarrow C=1000$, so $P(t)=1000e^{0.05t}$.

$$P(20)=1000e^{1}\approx 1000\times 2.718 \approx \boxed{2{,}718 \text{ individuals}}$$
Summary

What We've Learned

  1. Motivation: Integration recovers quantity from rate — the reverse of differentiation.
  2. Riemann Sums: Area approximated by $n$ rectangles; as $n\to\infty$, the sum converges to the definite integral.
  3. Antidifferentiation: $F$ is an antiderivative of $f$ iff $F'=f$; all antiderivatives differ only by a constant $C$.
  4. Indefinite Integral: $\int f(x)\,dx=F(x)+C$ — the complete family of antiderivatives.
  5. Four Core Rules: Constant, Power, Logarithmic ($n=-1$ case), and Exponential rules cover the majority of Calculus I integrals.
  6. Algebraic Rules: Constants factor out; integrals of sums/differences split term-by-term.
  7. Differential Equations: Separable DEs solved by separation of variables + integration + initial condition.
  8. Continuous Compounding: The IVP $\dfrac{dB}{dt}=rB$, $B(0)=P$ yields $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$ — derived, not memorized.
