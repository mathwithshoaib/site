§ 1 — The Big Question

You Know Derivatives.
Now, Let’s Work Backwards.

You have spent weeks mastering derivatives — the tool that captures instantaneous rates of change. The derivative of position is velocity; the derivative of velocity is acceleration; the derivative of profit is marginal profit.

Now consider the reverse question — perhaps the most natural question in all of mathematics: if I know only the rate of change, can I recover the original quantity? This question, simple to ask and profound to answer, is the soul of integration.

🚗 A Road-Trip Problem

Your car’s speedometer works perfectly, but the odometer is broken. You need to know: “How far have I traveled in the last 3 hours?”

You record your speed at half-hour intervals:

Best estimate: $(60+80+70+90+50+65)(0.5) = \mathbf{207.5 \text{ km}}$

But this is only an approximation. The speed changed continuously. Integration gives us the exact answer.

The Core Insight: If $v(t)$ is speed at time $t$, then the exact total distance from $t=a$ to $t=b$ is the area under the curve $y=v(t)$.
§ 2 — Where Integration Comes From

Riemann Sums: The Birth
of the Integral Sign

Picture slicing the shaded region under a curve into a hundred paper-thin vertical strips. Each strip is almost a perfect rectangle — you know its width precisely, and its height is just the value of the function at one chosen point. Sum every rectangle’s area, and you have a remarkably good approximation of the true area beneath the curve. Now imagine slicing into a thousand strips. A million. A trillion. Something extraordinary happens: as the strips grow infinitely thin, the approximation converges, with the certainty of mathematics, to a single perfect number. That number is the definite integral.

The elongated $\displaystyle\int$ symbol? Leibniz invented it in 1675 as a stretched letter S — for Summa, Latin for sum. And the $dx$ at the end? It is the ghost of $\Delta x$ as it shrinks to zero, whispering: “I am the infinitely thin width of each slice.”

  1. Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ equal sub-intervals, each of width $\Delta x = \dfrac{b-a}{n}$.
  2. Pick a sample point $x_i^*$ in each sub-interval.
  3. Build rectangles: width $\Delta x$, height $f(x_i^*)$.
  4. Sum the areas: $\displaystyle S_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$.
  5. Take the limit $n \to \infty$ — sum becomes exact.

Section 5.1

Indefinite Integration
& Differential Equations

Now that we understand what the integral means, we master the mechanics of computing it — and discover how integration unlocks a new class of equations that model the living, breathing world.

5.1a — Antidifferentiation

Antidifferentiation

If differentiation is the art of finding rates of change, antidifferentiation is the art of running backwards — recovering a function from its derivative.

Definition — Antiderivative

A function $F(x)$ is an antiderivative of $f(x)$ if $F'(x) = f(x)$ for all $x$.

Fundamental Property of Antiderivatives

Theorem

If $F(x)$ is an antiderivative of continuous $f(x)$, then every other antiderivative has the form $G(x) = F(x) + C$ for some constant $C$.

💡 Quick Proof

Let $H=G-F$. Then $H'=0$ everywhere, so $H$ is constant. Thus $G=F+C$. ∎

Example: $x^3$, $x^3+7$, $x^3-\pi$ all have derivative $3x^2$.

5.1c — Rules for Integrating Common Functions

Integration Rules

Rule 1 — The Constant Rule

$$\int k\,dx = kx + C$$

Since $\dfrac{d}{dx}(kx)=k$, the antiderivative of constant $k$ is $kx$.

Example 1. $\displaystyle\int 7\,dx = 7x + C$    Check: $\dfrac{d}{dx}(7x+C)=7$ ✓
Example 2. $\displaystyle\int (-4)\,dx = -4x + C$
$\displaystyle\int 0\,dx = C$

Rule 2 — The Power Rule

$$\int x^n\,dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \qquad (n \neq -1)$$

Raise the exponent by 1, divide by the new exponent.

Example 1. $\displaystyle\int x^5\,dx = \frac{x^6}{6} + C$
Example 2 (fractional). $\displaystyle\int \sqrt{x}\,dx = \frac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+C$
Example 3 (negative). $\displaystyle\int x^{-3}\,dx = -\frac{1}{2x^2}+C$
$\dfrac{4}{7}x^{7/4}+C$

Rule 3 — The Logarithmic Rule

$$\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C \qquad (x \neq 0)$$

The power rule fails for $n=-1$. Since $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln|x|=\dfrac{1}{x}$, the antiderivative is $\ln|x|$.

Example. $\displaystyle\int \frac{8}{x}\,dx = 8\ln|x|+C$

Rule 4 — The Exponential Rule

$$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C \qquad \text{and} \qquad \int e^{kx}\,dx = \frac{e^{kx}}{k}+C$$

$e^x$ is its own derivative — and its own antiderivative.

Example 1. $\displaystyle\int e^{3x}\,dx = \frac{e^{3x}}{3}+C$
Example 2. $\displaystyle\int 5e^{-2x}\,dx = -\frac{5}{2}e^{-2x}+C$
Algebraic Rules for Indefinite Integration
Constant Multiple Rule
$$\int k\cdot f(x)\,dx = k\int f(x)\,dx$$
Sum and Difference Rule
$$\int \bigl[f(x) \pm g(x)\bigr]\,dx = \int f(x)\,dx \pm \int g(x)\,dx$$

✏️ Example — Combining Rules

Find $\displaystyle\int\!\left(6x^2 - \frac{4}{x} + 3e^x\right)dx$.

$$= 2x^3 - 4\ln|x| + 3e^x + C$$

✏️ Example — Simplify First

Find $\displaystyle\int\frac{x^3 - 4x}{x}\,dx$.

$$\int(x^2-4)\,dx = \frac{x^3}{3}-4x+C$$

5.1d — Applications

Integration in Action

📈 Finding a Function from Its Slope

A curve passes through $(1,4)$ with slope $f'(x)=3x^2-2x+1$. Find $f(x)$.

$f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+C$. Apply $f(1)=4$: $C=3$.

$$f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+3$$

💰 Total Cost from Marginal Cost

$C'(x)=3x^2-12x+14$, fixed costs $\$35$. Find $C(x)$.

$C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+K$. $C(0)=35 \Rightarrow K=35$.

$$C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+35$$
5.1e — Introduction to Differential Equations

Differential Equations

A differential equation is an equation involving an unknown function and its derivatives — the language in which physics, biology, and economics write their deepest laws.

Definition

The order of a DE is the order of the highest derivative appearing. In Calc I we study separable equations where variables can be separated.

TypeFormNote
Separable$\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$Our focus in Calc I
Linear (1st order)$y'+P(x)y=Q(x)$Integrating factor method
Autonomous$dy/dt=f(y)$Right side depends only on $y$
Higher order$y''+4y=\sin x$Studied in Calc II / Diff. Eq.
Method — Separation of Variables

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)\cdot g(y)$:

  1. Separate: $\dfrac{dy}{g(y)}=f(x)\,dx$
  2. Integrate both sides
  3. Solve for $y$
  4. Apply initial condition to find $C$
Why It Works

Dividing by $g(y)$ and integrating w.r.t. $x$, using $dy=\dfrac{dy}{dx}dx$, converts the left side to $\int\dfrac{dy}{g(y)}$. Justified by FTC + Chain Rule.

💼 Revenue IVP

$\dfrac{dR}{dq}=50-2q$, $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$.

$R(q)=50q-q^2+C$. $R(0)=0 \Rightarrow C=0$.

$$R(q)=50q-q^2 \qquad \text{(max at } q=25\text{)}$$

🌿 Exponential Growth/Decay

Solve $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=ky$, $y(0)=y_0$.

$\dfrac{dy}{y}=k\,dx$ → $\ln|y|=kx+C_1$ → $y=Ce^{kx}$.

$$y=y_0 e^{kx}$$
5.1f — Continuous Compounding via Differential Equations

Continuous Compounding

🏠 The Model

$\dfrac{dB}{dt}=rB$ — the richer you are, the faster it grows. This is the DE of continuous compounding.

Theorem — Continuous Compounding Formula

If $P$ dollars are invested at annual rate $r$ compounded continuously:

$$B(t)=Pe^{rt}$$

✏️ Full Derivation

IVP: $\dfrac{dB}{dt}=rB$, $B(0)=P$.

$\dfrac{dB}{B}=r\,dt$ → $\ln|B|=rt+C_1$ → $B=Ce^{rt}$.

$B(0)=C=P$, therefore $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$ ✓

💵 Example

$\$5{,}000$ at $6\%$ continuous compounding for 10 years.

$$B(10)=5000e^{0.6}\approx\$9{,}110.60$$

$f'(x)=6x-4$, $f(2)=3$. Find $f(x)$.

Problem 4 — Marginal Revenue

$R'(q)=120-4q$, $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$ and maximising quantity.

$R(q)=120q-2q^2$. Max at $q=30$: $R(30)=\$1{,}800$.

Problem 5 — Continuous Compounding

How many years for $\$2{,}000$ to triple at $8\%$ continuous compounding?

$$t=\frac{\ln 3}{0.08}\approx 13.73 \text{ years}$$

Problem 6 — Challenge

$\dfrac{dP}{dt}=0.05P$, $P(0)=1000$. Find $P(t)$ and $P(20)$.

$P(t)=1000e^{0.05t}$.

$$P(20)=1000e\approx 2{,}718$$
