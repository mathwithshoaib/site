§ 1 — Motivation

You Know Derivatives. Now Work Backwards.

You've spent weeks mastering derivatives — the tool that captures instantaneous rates of change. Now flip the question: if I know only the rate of change, can I recover the original quantity?

🚗 Road-Trip Problem

Your speedometer works perfectly but the odometer is broken. You record your speed every 30 minutes. To find the exact distance, you need integration — not an approximation, the exact accumulated total.

Core Insight: The exact distance from $t=a$ to $t=b$ equals the area under the speed curve $y = v(t)$.
§ 2 — Riemann Sums

Where the Integral Sign Was Born

Imagine slicing the area under a curve into a hundred paper-thin strips. Each strip is almost a perfect rectangle — width $\Delta x$, height $f(x_i^*)$. Sum every rectangle's area. Now imagine a thousand strips. A trillion. As they grow infinitely thin, the sum converges to a single perfect number. That number is the definite integral. The $\int$ symbol is Leibniz's stretched S — for Summa, Latin for sum. The $dx$ is the ghost of $\Delta x$ shrinking to zero.

  1. Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ sub-intervals, $\Delta x = (b-a)/n$.
  2. Pick sample points $x_i^*$ in each sub-interval.
  3. Build rectangles of width $\Delta x$ and height $f(x_i^*)$.
  4. Sum the areas: $S_n = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$.
  5. Take the limit $n o\infty$ — the sum becomes exact.

§ 3 — Definite Integral

The Formal Definition

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx \;=\; \lim_{n o\infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$$

§ 4 — Fundamental Theorem

The Bridge Between Derivatives & Integrals

FTC — Evaluation Shortcut

If $F'(x) = f(x)$, then   $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b) - F(a)$

Find an antiderivative, plug in, subtract. No limits. No sums.

§ 5.1a — Antidifferentiation

Antiderivatives

Definition

$F(x)$ is an antiderivative of $f(x)$ if $F'(x)=f(x)$.

Fundamental Property

If $F(x)$ is one antiderivative of $f(x)$, every other has the form $G(x) = F(x) + C$.

Indefinite Integral
$$\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C$$

A family of functions, not a number. The $+C$ captures all antiderivatives simultaneously.

§ 5.1c — Integration Rules

The Four Core Rules

Rule 1 — Constant Rule

$$\int k\,dx = kx + C$$

Example: $\int 7\,dx = 7x + C$

Rule 2 — Power Rule

$$\int x^n\,dx = rac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \qquad (n eq-1)$$

Raise exponent by 1, divide by new exponent.
Ex 1: $\int x^5\,dx = \dfrac{x^6}{6}+C$  ·  Ex 2: $\int\sqrt{x}\,dx = \dfrac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+C$

$n=3/4$, new exp $=7/4$:   $\dfrac{4}{7}x^{7/4}+C$

Rule 3 — Logarithmic Rule

$$\int rac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C$$

The power rule fails for $n=-1$. Since $ rac{d}{dx}\ln|x|= rac{1}{x}$, the antiderivative is $\ln|x|$.

Rule 4 — Exponential Rule

$$\int e^x\,dx = e^x + C \qquad \int e^{kx}\,dx = rac{e^{kx}}{k}+C$$

$e^x$ is its own antiderivative. For $e^{kx}$, divide by $k$.

Algebraic Rules

Constant Multiple: $\int k\cdot f(x)\,dx = k\int f(x)\,dx$

Sum/Difference: $\int[f\pm g]\,dx = \int f\,dx\pm\int g\,dx$

✏️ Example — Combined Rules

$$= 2x^3 - 4\ln|x| + 3e^x + C$$
§ 5.1d — Applications

Integration in Action

📈 Finding a Function from Its Slope

$f'(x)=3x^2-2x+1$,   $f(1)=4$.   Find $f(x)$.

Integrate: $f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+C$

Apply IC: $4=1-1+1+C \Rightarrow C=3$

$$f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+3$$

💰 Total Cost from Marginal Cost

$C'(x)=3x^2-12x+14$, fixed costs $\$35$. Find $C(x)$.

$C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+K$

$C(0)=35\Rightarrow K=35$, so $C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+35$

§ 5.1e — Differential Equations

Separable Differential Equations

Separation of Variables

Given $\dfrac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$: separate → $\dfrac{dy}{g(y)}=f(x)\,dx$, integrate both sides, solve, apply IC.

💼 Revenue IVP

$\dfrac{dR}{dq}=50-2q$, $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$.

$R(q)=50q-q^2+C$.   $R(0)=0\Rightarrow C=0$.

$$R(q)=50q-q^2 \quad ext{(max at } q=25 ext{)}$$

§ 5.1f — Continuous Compounding

Deriving $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$

Continuous Compounding Formula

If $P$ dollars are invested at rate $r$ compounded continuously:

$$B(t)=Pe^{rt}$$

✏️ Full Derivation from the Differential Equation

IVP: $\dfrac{dB}{dt}=rB$, $B(0)=P$.

Separate: $\dfrac{dB}{B}=r\,dt$  →  $\ln|B|=rt+C_1$  →  $B=Ce^{rt}$

Apply IC: $C=P$, therefore $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$ ✓
