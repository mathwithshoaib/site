Indefinite Integration &
Differential Equations

From accumulating infinitely many infinitely small pieces — to the most powerful tool in calculus. Your journey into integration starts here.

§ 1 — Motivation

You Know Derivatives.
Now Work Backwards.

You've spent weeks mastering derivatives — the tool that captures instantaneous rates of change. Now flip the question: if I know only the rate of change, can I recover the original quantity?

🚗 Road-Trip Problem

Your speedometer works perfectly — but the odometer is broken. You know your speed $v(t)$ at every instant. How far did you travel from $t=a$ to $t=b$?

The answer is the area under the speed curve. Integration computes that area exactly.

Core Insight: If $v(t)$ is speed at time $t$, total distance $= \int_a^b v(t)\,dt$ = area under $y=v(t)$.
§ 2 — Where the Integral Sign Was Born

Riemann Sums

Imagine slicing the area under a curve into a hundred paper-thin strips. Each strip is almost a perfect rectangle — width $\Delta x$, height $f(x_i^*)$. Sum them all. Now imagine a million strips. As they shrink to zero width, the sum converges to a single perfect number. That number is the definite integral. The $\int$ symbol is Leibniz's stretched S — for Summa, Latin for sum.

  1. Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ sub-intervals, $\Delta x = (b-a)/n$.
  2. Pick sample points $x_i^*$ in each sub-interval.
  3. Build rectangles: width $\Delta x$, height $f(x_i^*)$.
  4. Sum: $S_n = \displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$.
  5. Limit $n o\infty$ — sum becomes exact.

§ 3 — The Bridge

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

$$\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = \lim_{n o\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*)\,\Delta x$$
FTC — Evaluation Shortcut

If $F'(x)=f(x)$, then $\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)$

Find an antiderivative, plug in, subtract. No limits. No infinite sums.

§ 4 — The Notation

The Indefinite Integral

Antiderivative

$F(x)$ is an antiderivative of $f(x)$ if $F'(x)=f(x)$. Every other antiderivative has the form $G(x)=F(x)+C$.

Indefinite Integral
$$\int f(x)\,dx = F(x) + C$$

A family of functions, not a number. The $+C$ captures all antiderivatives simultaneously.

§ 5 — Integration Rules

The Four Core Rules

Rule 1 — Constant Rule

$$\int k\,dx = kx + C$$

Example: $\int 7\,dx = 7x+C$

Rule 2 — Power Rule

$$\int x^n\,dx = rac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}+C \qquad (n eq-1)$$

Raise exponent by 1, divide by new exponent.
Ex: $\int x^5\,dx= rac{x^6}{6}+C$  ·  $\int\sqrt{x}\,dx= rac{2}{3}x^{3/2}+C$

$n= rac{3}{4}$, new exp $= rac{7}{4}$:   $ rac{4}{7}x^{7/4}+C$

Rule 3 — Logarithmic Rule

$$\int rac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x|+C$$

The power rule fails for $n=-1$. Since $ rac{d}{dx}\ln|x|= rac{1}{x}$, the antiderivative is $\ln|x|$.

Rule 4 — Exponential Rule

$$\int e^x\,dx = e^x+C \qquad \int e^{kx}\,dx= rac{e^{kx}}{k}+C$$

$e^x$ is its own antiderivative. For $e^{kx}$, divide by $k$.

Algebraic Rules

Constant Multiple: $\int kf(x)\,dx=k\int f(x)\,dx$

Sum/Difference: $\int[f\pm g]\,dx=\int f\,dx\pm\int g\,dx$

$$= 2x^3 - 4\ln|x| + 3e^x + C$$
§ 6 — Applications

Integration in Action

📈 Finding a Function from Its Slope

$f'(x)=3x^2-2x+1$, $f(1)=4$. Find $f(x)$.

$f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+C$. Apply $f(1)=4$: $C=3$.

$$f(x)=x^3-x^2+x+3$$

💰 Total Cost from Marginal Cost

$C'(x)=3x^2-12x+14$, fixed costs $35. Find $C(x)$.

$C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+K$. $C(0)=35\Rightarrow K=35$.

$$C(x)=x^3-6x^2+14x+35$$

§ 7 — Differential Equations

Separable Equations

Separation of Variables

Given $ rac{dy}{dx}=f(x)g(y)$: separate → $ rac{dy}{g(y)}=f(x)\,dx$, integrate both sides, solve, apply IC.

💼 Revenue IVP

$ rac{dR}{dq}=50-2q$, $R(0)=0$. Find $R(q)$.

$R(q)=50q-q^2+C$. $R(0)=0\Rightarrow C=0$.

$$R(q)=50q-q^2 \quad ( ext{max at }q=25)$$

§ 8 — Continuous Compounding

Deriving $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$

Continuous Compounding

Solve $ rac{dB}{dt}=rB$, $B(0)=P$ to get $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$.

✏️ Full Derivation

Separate: $ rac{dB}{B}=r\,dt$ → $\ln|B|=rt+C_1$ → $B=Ce^{rt}$. Apply IC: $C=P$.  ∴ $B(t)=Pe^{rt}$ ✓
